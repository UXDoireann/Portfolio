Introduction

Open water swimming is a sport that has seen a 'rising tide' of popularity in Ireland in recent times. Even before the pandemic, the health benefits - both mental and physical - along with the camaraderie and love for the water had increased the numbers of enthusiasts taking the plunge. Lockdown restrictions saw an accelerated wave of new swimmers, with news of the sport's new found favour even making waves offshore.

However, for swimmers looking to find out important information before a swim eg. tide times, sea conditions, water temperature and more - it can be difficult. Tide and sea apps are mostly aimed at other 'sea-activities' eg. sailing, surfing or fishing. I wanted to design an app just for open water swimmers to help them find the information they needed before a swim, to help them getting the best of of their swim, while staying safe.

Goal

A passion project I'm designing, the goal is create an app for open water swimmers to check tide times and sea conditions for a desired swim spot.

Research

Online Survey

Firstly, I created a short online survey to get a broad initial sense of how open water swimmers use websites or apps to find out required information before a swim, what kind of information they look for and what problems they experience in finding that information. Sharing this survey on open water swim groups on social media, I quickly amassed 265 responses.

  • Most swimmers used mobile apps or websites before their swim to check tides, check weather and/or connect with a swim buddy.

  • Apps used to check tides include: Magic Seaweed; Tides Near Me; High Tide; Tide Times.

  • Swimmers used more than one app to find information eg. one for weather, another for tides, etc

  • Problems experienced using tide apps include: being difficult to navigate; information being confusing or having to to alot of searching and scrolling.

Competitive Benchmarking

Following on from the online survey, I examined the apps swimmers had mentioned that they used, along with some other apps and websites. Comparing how these apps solved the problem of allowing users to find out tide information, I compiled an extensive competitive benchmarking document. I wanted to understand established conventions, learn from best practice and find out areas for improvement.

For each app, I examined:

  • The Landing Page: How easily I could find my nearest or desired location

  • Search Results: How well the information was presented

  • Other Features: If other useful information was also given.

Usability Tests

I conducted two usability tests on two regular open-water sea swimmers. Firstly, I interviewed them about their open water swimming habits: how they plan a swim; how they find out information in advance; who they go with; what the scenario was the last time they went for an open water swim.

I then ask them to check the tides and look for information for a swim using Tide Times Pro and Magic Seaweed. These were both apps that swimmers had mentioned they used during the survey and also rated highly in competitive benchmarking.

Analysis

Affinity Diagram

Using Miro, I collected all the data from the online survey, competitive benchmarking and usabiity tests. After writing down my observations on individual 'post-its', I then went through a process of grouping and re-grouping various bits of data. Getting quite granular, an order was eventually instilled on all the data for the next step.

Customer Journey Map

Following on from the affinity diagram, I then translated this colourful, 'ordered mess' into a readable deliverable. The customer - or as I prefer to say - the swimmer journey map charts the swimmer's journey from: their regular swim habits; the conversations and preparations they make before a swim; when they look for information; when they find the information; what they do after finding out the necessary information and before going for the swim. The overall flow here is quite short but it's very obvious there's a dip when swimmers initially go and look for information and then a more drastic dip when they are presented with search results from an app for a desired location.

Design

Flow Diagram

While the high level flow for this utility app isn't long, the two main steps have to be handled carefully and well. Firstly, from the research I've done, swimmers appreciate being shown a list of nearby swim locations to choose from. Since this app is aimed at open-water swimmers, this list will be of known 'swim locations' - and not locations that are unsafe. Alternative options for searching for a location are also catered for if no geolocation is available.

From research, swimmers want to have a brief look at the times of the tides for the week ahead first and then want to know more details depending once they can see a date and time that suit. Bearing this in mind, search results yield a brief overview of tide times for week ahead - each with a little chevron to indicate that the day can be opened for further information eg. wind, weather, sea & air temperature, sunset & sunrise times.

Interaction Design

I made sketches for the first iteration of the screen design. The primary flow sees the swimmer, having previously allowed GPS while using the app, being presented with a list of nearby swim locations. By choosing a location, they are then shown an overview of the tides for the next 6 days. By tapping a day, a window opens with detailed info. In this flow, the swimmer also wants to check what the tide type means, so they tap on the explainer icon.

1. After the app loads, the swimmer is presented with a list of nearby swim spots. As previously mentioned, they are recognised swim spots - places where swimmers are seen regularly.

The distance from the swimmer's current location to the swim spot is shown.

The menu bar allows for alternative swim search options: a list of all locations, ordered by county & an option to search by map.

2. The swimmer is then shown an overview of tide times for the next 6 days. According to my research - swimmers want to know the tide times first - to see if those times fit into their schedule and then want to know more information eg. conditions, weather.

Having drawn this first iteration, I'm concerned the screen is a little busy - it might overwhelm the first time user. However, I'd like to test it first in a medium fidelity prototype before assuming or changing anything yet.

Also, I think instead of 'Looking Ahead', it should be the selected location in the next iteration.

3. When the swimmer taps on a day, a window opens to show more detailed information:

-Tide type is shown eg. spring tide/ neap tide with a link to explanation.

-Weather icon along with sea & air temperature

-Wind information is given with icon and simple language.

-Sunrise & sunset times

-If water quality information is available, it's shown.

-Icon to share the info for that day.

This is a really important screen to get right - so I think I'll test and iterate it afew times.

4. Seeing the tide type ie. 'spring tide' in this case on the previous screen, the swimmer wants to check what this means. When they click 'spring tide' or the 'i' icon on the previous screen, this explainer screen pops up. It offers brief explanations of main tide types in Ireland - emphasising what it means for swimmers rather than much technical or scientific information.
