Flight Booking App

As part of a Professional Diploma in UX at the UX Design Institute, this project involved designing a mobile flight-booking app for a fictitious airline.

Designed to take out the confusion and regain confidence and trust in the flight-booking process - from user research, to analysis, to design, prototyping and wireframing, I gained experience of the full life cycle of a UX project.

Research

Competitive Benchmarking

To understand how best-in-class mobile apps solve the problem of creating a positive user experience of booking flights (or accommodation in the case of the indirect competitor, Airbnb), I compared and reviewed four apps:

  1. Turkish Airlines
  2. Alaska Air
  3. Easyjet
  4. Airbnb

I focussed on three segments of the experience: the landing page; flight/accommodation search; entering personal details.


View Full Competitive Benchmarking Document
Landing Page

Both Turkish Airlines and Alaska Air have great landing pages. Both have 'Hello' greetings, very clear and beautiful UI with 'no messing around' CTAs.

Airbnb's landing page is quite underwhelming and requires some scrolling to make sense of the image that greets you.

Flight Search

Apart from the links to some unnecessary features, Easyjet wins with a clear, intuitive flow. If there was unavailability, I was easily directed to alternatives.

While Turkish Airline's 'flexi-search' is innovative, it's quite confusing and counter-intuitive for the first-time user.

Passenger Details

Turkish Airlines offered a very clean, easy process with no mandatory sign in.

Easyjet ruined its clean, easy process with mandatory registering for an account.

Online Survey

I created an online survey to get a better understanding of people's goals when booking flights online and to find out:

  • If the last time they booked a flight, did they use a mobile app or desktop website?
  • Did they use an aggregator website as part of their search?
  • What website or mobile app did they use to make their booking and why?
  • Did this mobile app or website allow them to complete their booking easily?
  • If not, why not?

Usability Testing

I conducted three usability tests, each including an in-depth interview examining the user's habits and contexts surrounding their previous experiences of booking flights online. As FlyUX is a fictitious airline, I used two apps previously reviewed during Competitive Benchmarking: Turkish Airlines and Easyjet.

I examined in particularly how they experienced the flow from: the landing page, to flight search & selection, filling in passenger details, seat selection & extras, through to flight summary. Listening to and observing these people search for and book flights was an incredibly interesting experience and provided a trove of insights and ideas. Throughout the overall design process, I kept these people in mind, thinking of their goals, their behaviours, their frustrations, their delight, their skepticism, their surprise and their stories.

    • Customer Journey Map

    Following on from the in-depth analysis of the affinity diagram, the next step was to translate this 'wall art installation' into a clear, structured deliverable. From the decisions and conversations preceding opening an app to book a flight, to all the behaviour, decisions and emotions experienced while going through the process of searching for and booking flights, to the sharing of the booking afterwards - all are mapped here in this document. From a cursory glance at this document, it's easy to see that the customer experience dips drastically during flight and fare selection and again when it comes to seat selection and adding luggage and extras.

    View full customer journey map document

    Key Findings
    • Price clarity: Users have a general distrust and skepticism of 'extras' eg. seats and luggage costs and hence a need for a constant visible update of cost.

    • Guidance when lack of availability: As this project took place during Covid-19 pandemic, when searching for flights, lack of availability was often an issue. How an app handled this (or didn't!) was a key noteworthy point

    • Established conventions: Deviation from established conventions eg. in calendars or 'flexi-search' graphs often cause confusion for users

    • Unnecessary features: Innovative and novel ways of flight searching can be confusing or just irrelevant to users.

    • Forthcoming information: Important information should be more forthcoming eg. layovers

    • Option to edit: Users like if they can edit flight summary.

    • Abbreviations: There shouldn't be assummed familiarity.

    • Feeling of flow: Users appreciate when they can complete their desired tasks easily.

    • Nights away: 'Nights away' are understood more by users that 'days away'. It's helpful to update this somewhere if the dates change during selection from original search.

    • Continue as guest: Users don't like when there is a mandatory 'sign-in'.



    Design

    User Flow Diagram

    Addressing all the issues in the Customer Journey Map, the next step was to define the high-level flow for this complex and structured, but overall linear process mobile app. Focussing on the journey from searching for flights to payment, each screen, screen state and interaction is accounted for. Keeping in mind issues the user's raised, some points of note here are:

    • 'Nights away' listed on calendar rather than 'days away'.
    • Flow of guidance if flight route or date unavailable
    • Options to edit flight summary
    • Option to continue as guest without forced sign-in
    • No unnecessary features
    • Abbreviation explainers

I've included below here some screens of note. For the full pdf document, please press the link.


    View Interaction Design

    On the calendar after date selection, the number of nights away is noted. Nights rather than days are used - as I found days sometimes caused confusion for users.

    When there is a lack of availability on the chosen outbound date, recognisable conventions are used to guide the user to alternative options.

    Due to the lack of availability for their original outbound date, their chosen alternative dates now mean their trip is less nights away than previously planned. This can be important to many users who wish to maximise their time away.

    Basic details of any layovers must be flagged during flight selection. Also, the updated total cost must be forthcoming as much as possible.

    Consequences of not booking seats must be forthcoming so users can make an informed choice.

    No forced sign in, but an explanation of the advantages of signing up.

    Prototyping

    Medium Fidelity Prototype

    Using Adobe XD, I created a medium fidelity prototype. Deliberately defining the user flow to explore lack of availability on a desired date of the outbound flight, the following measures were implemented to test:

    1. On the calendar page, '14 nights away' is written at the bottom once outbound and return dates are selected.

    2. When the outbound date is unavailable, the user is presented with this information on a 'tab' screen, with clear options to swipe left or right for days before or after on tabs with their corresponding lowest prices. This is a recognisable design convention.

    3. In this scenario, the user chooses the date after their original desired date. As a result, their trip is now 13 nights instead of 14 nights as intended. This is flagged on top of the outbound date list-view screen once selected.

    4. The user then navigates to the following page and then 14 nights in Tokyo is written at the top of the list-view screen.

    5. In the Flight Summary screen, there are clear options to edit the trip

    6. Amidst the confusion, the user is now returning to a different airport in London than the outbound flight. This is also flagged in a pop-up screen when the user has pressed 'Continue' in Flight Summary screen.

    Usability Test

    I undertook a usability test to test this prototype flow, exploiting the opportunity to also conduct another indepth interview with a user. Overall, the user found the prototype very easy to use, with a good general flow and corresponding mental models with regards to the implemented features relating to availability.

    I did find one important detail I had to change in the next iteration. Presented with the list-view of available flights for a specific day, when the user selects a flight, that tab opens to reveal more details about the flight eg. layovers, available fares, etc. However, once the user selected the flight on the list-view, they thought they were now on the return flight screen. I realised that I needed to think about small visual adjustments so that it was obvious that once a tab on the list-view was selected that it opened a window with 'details'. A simple animated chevron would probably work....

    Iterate

    UI Design & High Fidelity Prototype.

    Following from the usability test, for the next iteration, the first issue to be addressed was to implement animated chevrons on list-view options for both flights and extras. Other implementations include:

    • Attractive destination images on Landing Page, 'Sign In' page and on Confirmation screen.

    • Animations for when flight search results are loading and for when payment is being processed.

    • 'Flagged' information - that may be 'pain points' highlighted in yellow.

    • Design and colour consistency throughout the screens.

    • Friendly 'encouraging' language during payment screens eg. You're almost on your way to Tokyo!

    • A constantly updated cost at the bottom of the screen.

    • A clear option to 'Continue As Guest' during 'Sign In' screen.

    • Before seat selection, it is clear that the user will be randomly assigned seating if seats not selected.

    Usability Test

    For this test, I wanted to see how the list-view pages (now with animated chevrons!) would correspond to the user's mental models. They worked! The user understood easily that windows were opening and that they were now being shown 'details'.

    Also, I wanted to make sure the UI design enchanced rather than hindered the overall flow. The results were positive and user could easily navigate through the 'lack of availability' with understanding of what was happening. Having the constantly updated price at the bottom and an option to 'Continue As Guest' were also appreciated.

    Conclusion

    What worked well

    Overall, the flight-booking process performed very well during the usability tests, with users navigating through the flow very easily.

    • Constant price clarity with the running cost consistently being updated at the bottom aided trust and confidence for the user - especially during seat selection and luggage. They knew there would be no nasty surprises!

    • Unavailability was handled with proper guidance, so users could easily navigate to days before or after the original date. Other corresponding details were also handled eg. overall time away being flagged so they could make the necessary decisions.

    • Important information was forthcoming eg. layovers, names of airports instead of solely abbreviations and what's included in each fare.

    • Users weren't forced to 'sign in' but could 'Continue As Guest'.

    • I learned the importance of small visual cues eg. animated chevrons, loading animations, etc - which sound really too obvious to mention - but they really assist a user's mental models in a big way. The attention to detail regarding the UI in the final presented prototype demonstrates this very well.


    What I would do differently

    I'm very pleased with the final prototype, but if things to consider are:

    • The 'edit' button on the Flight Summary page could be replaced with a pencil icon as I feel it's a little bit of text too much.

    • While the flow here relates to unavailable for a given day, I would like to consider how to manage this if there was no availability for the surrounding days or during a period of time at the desired destination airport. Situations like these arise frequently in our current Covid-19 era, with a constant need for updates on certain availability. It would be interesting to explore this further given different situations and flows.
