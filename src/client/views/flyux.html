Flight Booking App

As part of a Professional Diploma in UX at the UX Design Institute, this project involved designing a mobile flight-booking app for a fictitious airline.

Designed to take out the confusion and regain confidence and trust in the flight-booking process - from user research, to analysis, to design, prototyping and wireframing, I gained experience of the full life cycle of a UX project.

Research

Competitive Benchmarking

To understand how best-in-class mobile apps solve the problem of creating a positive user experience of booking flights (or accommodation in the case of the indirect competitor, Airbnb), I compared and reviewed four apps:

  1. Turkish Airlines
  2. Alaska Air
  3. Easyjet
  4. Airbnb

I focussed on three segments of the experience: the landing page; flight/accommodation search; entering personal details.


View Full Competitive Benchmarking Document
Landing Page

Both Turkish Airlines and Alaska Air have great landing pages. Both have 'Hello' greetings, very clear and beautiful UI with 'no messing around' CTAs.

Airbnb's landing page is quite underwhelming and requires some scrolling to make sense of the image that greets you.

Flight Search

Apart from the links to some unnecessary features, Easyjet wins with a clear, intuitive flow. If there was unavailability, I was easily directed to alternatives.

While Turkish Airline's 'flexi-search' is innovative, it's quite confusing and counter-intuitive for the first-time user.

Passenger Details

Turkish Airlines offered a very clean, easy process with no mandatory sign in.

Easyjet ruined its clean, easy process with mandatory registering for an account.

Online Survey

I created an online survey to get a better understanding of people's goals when booking flights online and to find out:

  • If the last time they booked a flight, did they use a mobile app or desktop website?
  • Did they use an aggregator website as part of their search?
  • What website or mobile app did they use to make their booking and why?
  • Did this mobile app or website allow them to complete their booking easily?
  • If not, why not?

Usability Testing

I conducted three usability tests, each including an in-depth interview examining the user's habits and contexts surrounding their previous experiences of booking flights online. As FlyUX is a fictitious airline, I used two apps previously reviewed during Competitive Benchmarking: Turkish Airlines and Easyjet.

Listening to and observing these people search for and book flights was an incredibly interesting experience and provided a trove of insights and ideas. Throughout the overall design process, I kept these people in mind, thinking of their goals, their behaviours, their frustrations, their delight, their skepticism, their surprise and their stories.

    • Customer Journey Map

    Following on from the in-depth analysis of the affinity diagram, the next step was to translate this 'wall art installation' into a clear, structured deliverable. From the decisions and conversations preceding opening an app to book a flight, to all the behaviour, decisions and emotions experienced while going through the process of searching for and booking flights, to the sharing of the booking afterwards - all are mapped here in this document. Work was done for a clear runway, from which to 'take off' with the next step of designing...

    View full customer journey map document

    Key Findings
    • Users have a general distrust and skepticism of 'extras' and luggage costs and hence a need for a constant price clarity

    • As this project took place during Covid-19 pandemic, when searching for flights, lack of availability was often an issue. How an app handled this (or didn't!) was a key noteworthy point

    • Deviation from established conventions often caused confusion for users

    • Important information should be more forthcoming eg. layovers

    • Users don't like when there is a mandatory 'sign-in'.



    Step 3: Design

    User Flow Diagram

    Addressing all the issues in the Customer Journey Map, the next step was to define the high-level flow for this complex and structured, but overall linear process mobile app. Focussing on the journey from searching for flights to payment, each screen, screen state and interaction is accounted for. Keeping in mind issues the user's raised, some points of note here are:

    • Flow of guidance if flight route or date unavailable
    • Options to edit flight selection
    • Option to continue as guest without forced sign-in

    Interaction Design

    Drawing from the Flow Diagram, I really enjoyed sketching the screens and screen states. As my background is in visual art, I really felt at home problem-solving through sketching - always being aware that this is the first iteration of a number of iterations. I've included below here some screens of note. For the full pdf document, please press the link.


    On the calendar after date selection, the number of nights away is noted. Nights rather than days are used - as I found days sometimes caused confusion for users.

    When there is a lack of availability on the chosen outbound date, recognisable conventions are used to guide the user to alternative options.

    Due to the lack of availability for their original outbound date, their chosen alternative dates now mean their trip is less nights away than previously planned. This can be important to many users who wish to maximise their time away.

    Basic details of any layovers must be flagged during flight selection. Also, the updated total cost must be forthcoming as much as possible.

    Consequences of not booking seats must be forthcoming so users can make an informed choice.

    No forced sign in, but an explanation of the advantages of signing up.

    Step 4: Prototyping

    Medium Fidelity Prototype

    A protoype isn't a prototype without a user to test it. Upon completing a medium-fidelity prototype using Adobe XD, I undertook a usability test, exploiting the opportunity to also conduct another indepth interview. Overall, the user found the prototype very easy to use, with a good general flow. I took note of some minor improvements that could be made during the next iteration to further improve the flow. I was thinking little details eg. chevrons that animate to crosses when sections are opened and closed - which might sound trivial, but those visual cues really help people intuitively understand what's happening.     UI Design and Final Prototype

    For the final prototype, I wanted to finish it with some beautiful UI for consistency, polish and aesthetic appeal. Following this, I conducted one more user test. This was really a sanity check to fully confirm everything was working as required, but also to test the visual style too. Everything was working as expected and the user found it easy to use. Small visual nudges using animations and images further enhanced the flow. Please see image of screens below or click on the link to try the prototype yourself.

    Outcomes

    Flight booking can be a anxiety inducing experience for many people. Being forthcoming with key information throughout the process is really important in gaining trust with the person using the app. Throughout the design process, I constantly thought back to the usability tests. Design descisions all have their root in what these people expressed while trying to book a flight. The final prototype is really made for them. Working on this project made me realise how much my skills from my previous career really aligned with those required in a UX project, from talking to and listening to people, to reflecting on gathered research, to crafting that research into a design and utilising visual skills in honing the details of that design to assist the person interacting with it.
